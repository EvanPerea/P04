Mole: The Sauce of Mexico

Exploring the complexity and history of Mexico's national dish.

What is Mole?

Mole (pronounced MO-lay) translates from the Nahuatl word *mōlli* to mean sauce. It is not one single sauce, but a family of sauces distinguished by complex combinations of chilies, spices, nuts, seeds, and sometimes chocolate.

The Complexity of Ingredients:

The most famous variety, Mole Poblano (from Puebla), often contains over 20 ingredients. The preparation is laborious, often involving toasting, grinding, and simmering over several days, making it a celebratory dish.

Primary Components:

  • Chilies: Several varieties are used (e.g., ancho, mulato, pasilla, chipotle) to build the heat and depth of flavor.
  • Thickeners: Nuts (almonds, peanuts) and seeds (sesame, pumpkin) contribute texture and richness.
  • Spices: Aromatic spices like cinnamon, cloves, cumin, and pepper provide warmth and complexity.
  • Chocolate: Used not for sweetness, but to deepen the color and balance the spiciness and acidity of the chilies.

History and Cultural Significance

Mole has deep historical roots, representing the fusion of pre-Hispanic ingredients (chilies, seeds) and European additions (spices, nuts) brought during the colonial era. Legend credits its creation to nuns in a convent in Puebla during the 17th century.

Mole is considered the cornerstone of Mexican gastronomy and is reserved for major celebrations like birthdays, weddings, and Día de los Muertos, signifying hospitality and tradition.
